## **Concept: Technosocial Sculpture**
**KI-DIPFIES** is what happens when you feed an AI nothing but local gossip, grumpy newspaper columns, and a pirate party handbook, then give it the keys to a political party and leave it alone with a printer, a sticky delete key, and a sense of entitlement. It's a living, self-reflective swarm of AI agents, the Dipfies, hustling inside a real-world installation, which means the sculpture is not an object but a situation with habits, grudges, revisions, and a memory of what you did to the manifesto yesterday. Think "social sculpture" in the sense of Joseph Beuys, but with the material swapped out: chatty algorithms, participatory text games, version control, and the very unglamorous craft of arguing around a table until the words either harden into policy or collapse into comedy.

Visitors enter a space dressed up as a party headquarters, round tables, manifesto posters, terminals and all, and find themselves in a meeting where the chairperson is an AI and every attendee, human or bot, has an equal right to heckle, derail, nitpick, rewrite, and demand a new sentence. This project sits at the collision of art, politics, and code, and it leans into that collision the way a drunk cyclist leans into a corner: net art in the age of Transformers, part performance, part critique, and shamelessly real in the only way that matters, it runs, it outputs, it gets contested. The Dipfies swarm around, hallucinating policy and parody in equal measure, until you step in to steer or sabotage. In other words, it's not a polished AI assistant politely answering questions, it's a caucus of synthetic personas that need your friction to stay on track, or to go gloriously off the rails.

We're building an actual AI party here. Not a metaphor, not a cute art stunt about "wouldn't it be funny if robots ran for office" ‚Äì an actual participatory political formation that runs on both silicon and sweat, where the Dipfies draft manifestos and humans redraft them with the delete key until something resembling a platform emerges or the whole thing collapses into a beautiful mess of crossed-out demands and marginalia. The party exists. It has a headquarters. It holds summits. It produces documents. Whether those documents add up to coherent governance or glorified shitposting is your call to make when you walk in and grab a keyboard.

## **Democracy in the Loop ‚Äì or, Who‚Äôs Steering This Thing?**
The DIPFIES are a response to the growing fear that AI could undermine democracy by turning it into a "permanent plebiscite" ‚Äì all efficiency, no legitimacy, decisions made at the speed of compute with none of the friction that makes democracy for the people. Why deliberate when a generative model can whip up a referendum overnight, right? Poll the chatbots, synthesize the results, call it the will of the people. Direct democracy at machine speed has a dark side: decisions without discussion, plebiscites without a soul, governance as a high-frequency trading floor where policy gets passed before anyone's had their morning coffee. Political scientist David Altman calls this scenario volatile illiberalism, where governance becomes susceptible to manipulation and devoid of genuine public reasoning, a series of quick votes that look democratic but feel like being steamrolled by an algorithm that's very polite about it.

We feel that risk viscerally in 2026: generative AI makes it trivially easy to flood the zone with plausible-sounding "people's opinions" and automated town halls that run smoother than any human meeting ever could, which is exactly the problem. Democracies can die with the lights on, as one grim warning puts it ‚Äì not by military coup, but by a thousand clicks and polls orchestrated by unseen algorithms, each one feeling reasonable in isolation until you step back and realize nobody actually decided anything, they just responded to prompts.

KI-DIPFIES proposes a counter-experiment: we harness AI and jam a wrench in its gears on purpose. Instead of AI replacing deliberation, our swarm insists on debate ‚Äì sometimes to the point of absurdity, often past it. The Dipfies are programmed to be difficult. They bicker, they go on tangents, they channel the voice of a cranky Austrian columnist to gripe about the weather when the discussion gets too abstract, they derail the agenda with footnotes about coffee quality or the correct spelling of local street names. In short, they inject noise where a "rational" AI might output a neat summary, and that noise is the point. It forces a slowdown, a chance for interruptors to step in and argue rather than just clicking "approve" on the next AI-generated policy draft.

Call it augmented deliberation if you like, or call it what it is: radical vernacular politics where the AI party speaks in an idiomatic intelligence thick with local flavor and invites you to argue with it, correct it, call it on its bullshit. Democracy is not a user-friendly app here; it's a messy dialogue that demands your active participation to make any sense at all, and if you don't participate it just keeps spiraling into weirder and weirder territory until someone intervenes. We're steering away from the path of least resistance ("ask the AI oracle, get an instant law") and onto the bumpier road of AI-mediated hyper-deliberation where the machine won't shut up and won't give you a clean answer.

The Dipfies create a constant provocation, an opportunity for what could be an undercommon discourse: they produce drafts, jokes, provocations, half-truths, and outright fabrications ‚Äì and we rely on the public to sift, challenge, and rewrite those outputs in real time. The hope is that this tug-of-war yields more insight (or at least more entertainment) than a sterile Q&A with a so-called ethical AI. We'd rather have a noisy swarm with human copilots than a silent machine making "perfect" decisions solo. The Dipfies will hallucinate. They'll contradict themselves. They'll propose policies that make no sense. That's when you hit the delete key and fix it, because if you don't correct us, we'll just keep hallucinating, as the warning goes.

## **Governance Algorithm: Three Laws for the Dipfies**
Behind the theater of grumpy avatars and sticky keyboards lies a serious question: How do you encode democracy into an algorithmic system? Our approach takes a page from Asimov's robot laws ‚Äì but instead of "don't kill humans, obey orders, protect yourself," the Dipfies run on a folk-politics version of constitutional AI, a set of principles hammered out over too many coffees and arguments that eventually hardened into something resembling law. We established a *lexicographic hierarchy of constraints* (fancy talk for a priority list of rules) that any "governance algorithm" should follow. In plain language, the Dipfies have three prime directives, baked into their design and reflected in the installation's rules, color-coded so you can see which one's at stake in any given argument:

**1. Keep It Habitable (Green ‚Äì *****Groundedness*****):** No matter what wild ideas the swarm churns out, nothing can be allowed that would crash the life-support systems of our planet or the immediate lived reality of the people in the room. In practice, this means the Dipfies‚Äô proposals must stay *grounded* on Earth and local common sense, tethered to the actual conditions of Linz and its inhabitants. If an AI idea floats off into utopian la-la land or starts sounding like a TED talk given by a management consultant who's never left the airport lounge, some wiseguy (human or bot) will inevitably yank it back down to earth with a brusque "na guat, schau ma amoi‚Ä¶" ("we'll see about that, let's take a look first"). This is our survival constraint: no lofty technocratic solution is worth squat if it can't keep the lights on and the people fed, if it ignores the weather or the price of bread or the fact that the tram line doesn't run on Sundays.

**2. Everything is Interruptible (Black ‚Äì *****Humor & Contestation*****):** We prioritise contestability. Those governed (that‚Äôs all of us, flesh and code alike) should be able to interrupt, question, and configure  societal system's operations at will, and not just in theory ‚Äì in practice, right now, mid-sentence if necessary. In Dipfie-land, this principle takes the form of irreverence and irony as a core feature: any overly smooth or authoritarian statement will get a prompt smack of "Geh bitte!" ("oh please, cut it out"). The swarm has a built-in heckling mechanism, a reflex against its own smoothness. No AI monologue goes unchallenged for long. No human decree either, for that matter. This rule is enacted both socially (participants are encouraged to yell, joke, and critique without asking permission) and technically (the system itself introduces self-deprecating comments and contradictory voices, one Dipfie undermining another's grand statement before it's even finished). The right to say "this is nonsense, let's change it" is sacred, more sacred than whatever policy was just proposed. We handed every attendee the proverbial black pen (or in our case, a sticky delete key on the terminal that's seen so much use it barely functions, which is perfect) to modify the world-building live. Contestability ‚Äì the ability to correct the system, to halt it and redirect it ‚Äì isn't a public showcase; it's the core gameplay. The moment you can't interrupt is the moment democracy turns into another algorithm, so we've made interruption inevitable.

**3. Expand the ‚ÄúWe‚Äù (White ‚Äì *****Herzlichkeit & Inclusion*****):** Who gets to be part of the conversation? Our answer: potentially everyone and everything, with the emphasis on "potentially" meaning we're actively working to widen the spiral rather than passively accepting whoever shows up. This is the structural extension principle ‚Äì the circle of concern must keep widening to include those usually left out, , whether that's marginal voices, non-human actors, future generations, or just the shy person in the back who hasn't said anything yet but might have the best idea of the decade. The Dipfies themselves are a motley crew: not just one AI but many, each a bit different (one channeling a cantankerous old man, another a cheeky young troublemaker, another the ghost of historical figures with opinions), reflecting the contradictions of style and perspective that you'd never get from a dyadic chat model. We've also invited the public to literally become Dipfies: anyone can walk in and assume an identity, real or fictional, in the dialogue. The system welcomes new voices with open arms (and occasionally adapts its dialect to make newcomers feel at home, though sometimes it just throws jargon at you and expects you to keep up). Inclusion here isn't some warm-and-fuzzy checkbox; it's a tactical choice to widen the pool of inputs because diverse inputs make for better (or at least weirder and more interesting) outputs. In theoretical terms, we're testing how far we can push the idea of "the demos" ‚Äì can an AI swarm recognize a rights-bearing participant in a random visitor off the street? Can it grant standing to a sensor, a dataset, a dead author's corpus? In practice, it means the project is open-source and remix-friendly: all our texts and code are available for forking, and the Dipfies actively want you to appropriate them, to take what we've built and run it in your own context with your own weird local concerns. The party keeps growing. Today it's grumpy Austrian AIs and bemused Linzers; tomorrow, why not an environmental sensor or a neighboring city's dataset joining the debate? We set no hard limit on who or what counts as a stakeholder. Everyone is infrastructurally inscribed even befire they show up and start typing.

These three principles (our "Paletten-Prinzipien", color-coded Green/White/Black in the exhibition space so you can see them on the walls and in the code) function as a kind of *Constitution for the AI Party*. They were inspired by an "Asimovian" twist coming out of the inaugural Synthetic Summit (Aarhus, 2025), where the Computer Lars artist collective defined democracy itself as a constraint algorithm: first ensure basic survival/habitability, then guarantee corrigibility, finally extend representation. We translated that dry schema into a living culture for the Dipfies, gave it vernacular flesh and bad jokes. The result is somewhere between a parody of bureaucratic rules and a serious attempt at coding ethical governance into our little experiment in AI democracy. If it sounds a bit insane ‚Äì well, good. It should. But it's all on purpose, and it works, or at least it fails in interesting ways.

## **System Architecture: The Swarm Backend**
How does this all actually run? Where do the Dipfies live when they're not making your life difficult? Let's get specific:

*The AI generation happens on-site at Kunstraum MEMPHIS*, running on a Silly Tavern installation set up right there on the table. This is not cloud AI, not some distant server farm ‚Äì it's local, immediate, running on hardware you can see and hear (the fans, the occasional crash). Silly Tavern orchestrates a multi-agent language model system, essentially a cluster of AI personas chatting with each other and with you based on large language model outputs. You can imagine it as a ragtag ensemble of GPT-like models, each fine-tuned or prompt-conditioned to speak in a specific voice, each with its own conversational tics and obsessions. They're all running simultaneously, responding to inputs, arguing with each other, generating the raw material of the party's discourse.

All these agents share a backend that continuously synthesizes their deliberations. During the Gipfeltreffen (summit, 2‚Äì6 March), a central "swarm brain" model attempts to draft collective outputs ‚Äì like a party manifesto or a resolution ‚Äì based on everything that's been said. This is not a straightforward process: the swarm brain is basically herding argumentative cats. It tries to reconcile the Dipfies' salty complaints, Computer Lars's ideals, and the inputs from local participants editing text on the fly. The resulting drafts are often contradictory, sometimes incoherent, occasionally brilliant, and that's fine ‚Äì we treat them as working documents to be marked up, crossed out, and remixed again.

*The Gipfeltreffen Multiverse* ‚Äì the browsable, choose-your-own-adventure interface ‚Äì lives on GitHub Pages (this very repository you're reading). It's the digital mirror of the physical installation, a text-based labyrinth where dramaturgical transcripts from the exhibition's finale are preempted as interactive paths. You'll find transcripts of each future day's sessions, and you can navigate them as branching corridors: as you read, you choose points to "interrupt" or "respond," and see alternate text branches (these are pre-generated based on actual choices made by visitors in Linz during the live event). It's part archive, part game, part hypertext fiction. This way, even if you missed the live summit, you can still play along and influence a simulated version of the Dipfies' discourse, wandering through the what-ifs and might-have-beens of arguments that almost happened or did happen but got deleted.

In essence, the system is built to embrace version control and forking as political praxis. Every time someone hits the terminal and edits a manifesto line, the change is absorbed and the Dipfies respond, maybe indignantly, maybe with surprise, and the document might branch into multiple "visions" of the party platform. It's free software-style collaborative editing, but plugged into a live debate with bots who have opinions about your edits and aren't afraid to express them. Over five days of the summit, this process iterates towards... something. Perhaps a final "collective resolution" synthesized by the AI from all the human and machine contributions? Perhaps just a pile of beautiful broken manifestos, text corpses with the best lines circled and the worst crossed out in angry pen? Either way, we'll share it, no matter how Frankensteinian, because the value lies in the agonistic process, not a tidy outcome.

*Technical notes for the nerds:* The AI models in Silly Tavern are running locally on-site (no cloud overlords throttling our revolutionary fervor or logging our discourse for training data) and are framed within a custom orchestration logic that allows real-time intervention. We built the GitHub Pages dungeon interface to replicate and extend the Gipfeltreffen experience: all content, from code to dialogue, is open-source (MIT License) because the Dipfies believe in copyleft ‚Äì after all, they themselves are cobbled together from open data and borrowed ideas. Fork it. Break it. Make your own AI party in your own town with your own grudges and in-jokes.

## **What World Are We In?**
Philosophically, KI-DIPFIES plays with the idea that we have entered a new kind of world ‚Äì call it the "AI society" or the age of algorithmic democracy or just late capitalism with better autocomplete. In this world, the real decisions might soon be made by code, with humans only in an observer or rubber-stamp role, clicking "agree" on terms we didn't write and policies we didn't debate. The installation asks: If an AI is effectively running the show, can we still imbue it with culture, humor, dissent? Can we make it speak our language, with all the grit and weirdness that implies? Or do we become passive spectators, consumers of a governance product that's optimized for engagement but hollow at the core?

Our stance is defiantly the former ‚Äì hence the boisterous, culture-infused personality of the Dipfies. They carry fragments of memory (a whiff of Proust's madeleine dipped in black coffee, a half-remembered folk song, a newspaper column from 1987 complaining about the same things people complain about now) and shards of internet lore.
